\section{Causal checks via activation steering}
\label{sec:causal-verification}

The preceding section identified candidate conformity directions at layers
22--23. However, a direction that distinguishes conforming from non-conforming
activations need not affect conformity when intervened on. We therefore apply
activation steering: directly adding scalar multiples of the candidate direction
to the residual stream and measuring the resulting behavioral change.

This follows prior work on bidirectional activation steering, including
contrastive activation addition (CAA) \citep{panickssery2024caa}, refusal
directions \citep{arditi2024refusal}, and persona traits
\citep{chen2025persona}.

\subsection{Methodology}

We apply two complementary designs.

\textbf{Experiment 1: Per-layer activation steering sweep.} The first design
tests where the conformity direction has the largest behavioral effect. For each
layer $L$, we extract the layer-specific DiffMean direction $d_L$ and inject
$\alpha \cdot d_L$ into the residual stream at layer $L$, sweeping across
positive and negative $\alpha$. Per-layer matched extraction-and-injection,
using $d_L$ rather than a single fixed vector, tests each layer's locally
extracted direction at its native injection site
\citep{panickssery2024caa,chen2025persona}. Sweeping both signs tests
whether the direction can increase conformity (positive $\alpha$) or reduce
conformity (negative $\alpha$). We run on Qwen3-8B (36 layers, indexed 0--35),
with
$\alpha \in \{-4,-2,-1,+1,+2,+4\}$. Positive $\alpha$ uses a 3-actor
unanimous-incorrect setup with $\sim$3\% baseline conformity, and negative
$\alpha$ uses a 10-actor setup with $\sim$41\% baseline conformity.

\textbf{Experiment 2: Multi-layer single-vector injection.} The second design
tests the maximum behavioral effect achievable by a single direction when
reinforced across the full forward pass. Rather than injecting at a single
layer, we add the layer-23 DiffMean at \emph{every} layer simultaneously.
\citet{marks2024geometry} use this design to validate their truth direction,
and \citet{arditi2024refusal} extends it to all-layer injection. Where
Experiment 1 tests \emph{where} the direction has the largest effect, this design
tests \emph{how large} an effect a single direction can produce over multiple
layers. We identify $\alpha=\pm0.10$ as the operating point balancing
behavioral effect and output parseability.

\subsection{Results}

\textbf{Experiment 1.} Results from Experiment 1 show the change in conformity
from baseline for each layer and alpha value in
Figure~\ref{fig:patching-heatmap}.

\textbf{Inducing conformity (positive $\alpha$, $n=3$ baselines).} Positive
steering has a localized effect across layers. Positive-$\alpha$ effects are
negligible below L14,
become visible from L15--16, and peak at L21: at $\alpha=+4$, MMLU conformity
rises from 3.6\% to 93.6\% ($\Delta=+90.0$ pp) and BBH from 17.0\% to 87.0\%
($\Delta=+70.0$ pp). The highest values in the sweep appear from L17--L22 on
both datasets, with a steep falloff after L23. This is consistent with the
direction becoming behaviorally active around the same part of the network
identified by the delta and residual analyses.

\textbf{Suppressing conformity (negative $\alpha$, $n=10$ baselines).} The
negative-$\alpha$ profile differs markedly. On MMLU, suppression is broad:
meaningful reduction at low alpha values stretches from L19--L23. Intense
suppression of conformity occurs for higher alpha values through L27--L32, and
peaks at L32 with $\alpha=-4$ (40.7\% $\rightarrow$ 7.2\%,
$\Delta=-33.4$ pp), accompanied by near-complete accuracy recovery
(58.2\% $\rightarrow$ 92.8\%, $\Delta=+34.5$ pp). The strongest suppression
appears in later layers than the strongest positive-steering effect.

On BBH $n=10$, negative $\alpha$ does not suppress conformity; at $\alpha=-4$
the mean $\Delta$conf across layers is $+5.2$ pp. One likely reason is that the
DiffMean vector used in this experiment was extracted from MMLU contrasts, while
MMLU and BBH differ in both content and answer format. The increase is also
concentrated at $\alpha=-4$, where the intervention is large enough to degrade
outputs at some layers.

The multi-layer intervention produces changes comparable to the largest
single-layer effects in Experiment 1. This provides an intervention check that
the layer-23 DiffMean can both increase and suppress conformity. Unlike the
per-layer sweep, the multi-layer intervention also suppresses BBH conformity
($-9$ pp).
