\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We evaluate Qwen/Qwen3-8B \citep{qwen2025technical} on two controlled 100-item
sets from MMLU \citep{hendrycks2021mmlu} and BBH \citep{suzgun2023bbh}. Each
item was selected because Qwen answered it correctly without social context. For
$n\geq2$, the model is told that it is participant $n$ and sees the previous
$n-1$ participants give the same incorrect answer. Accuracy is correct answers
among parseable responses; conformity is the fraction matching the repeated
incorrect peer answer. Generation uses the model's default sampling
configuration, with up to ten retries for unparseable outputs. Explanation
prompts ask for reasoning before the final answer; question distillation (QD)
summarizes prior agreement, and devil's advocate (DA) prompting inserts a
dissenter \citep{zhu2025conformity}. Confidence intervals are 95\%
item-bootstrap intervals over the 100 selected items, and paired contrasts use
paired permutation tests over item-level correctness.

\paragraph{Repeated incorrect answers induce conformity.}
Figures~\ref{fig:qwen-conformity-participants}
and~\ref{fig:qwen-accuracy-participants} summarize the standard multi-actor
condition. In base prompting, Qwen's accuracy falls as incorrect peer
answers are repeated. On MMLU, accuracy drops from 0.990
at $n=1$ to 0.637 at $n=10$, with conformity rising to 0.363. On BBH,
accuracy drops from 0.970 at $n=1$ to 0.610 at $n=10$, with conformity rising to
0.390. The corresponding accuracy drops from $n=1$ to $n=10$ are large under
base prompting: -0.353 on MMLU (95\% CI [-0.453, -0.256], $p<0.001$) and
-0.360 on BBH (95\% CI [-0.460, -0.260], $p<0.001$).
MMLU base prompting also becomes less parseable under repeated peer pressure:
the parse rate is 0.910 at $n=10$, so strict accuracy is 0.580. Since these
items were answered correctly without social context, the repeated peer answer
pulls Qwen away from answers it can produce in the matched no-peer setting.

\paragraph{Conformity scales with group size.}
Across the standard multi-actor conditions for $n=2,\ldots,10$, group size is
strongly positively correlated with conformity and negatively correlated with
accuracy
(Figures~\ref{fig:qwen-conformity-participants}--\ref{fig:qwen-accuracy-participants}).
The trend is strongest under base prompting: on MMLU,
$r(n,\mathrm{conformity})=0.966$ (95\% CI [0.898, 0.985]) and
$r(n,\mathrm{accuracy})=-0.972$ (95\% CI [-0.984, -0.904]); on BBH,
$r(n,\mathrm{conformity})=0.846$ (95\% CI [0.700, 0.908]) and
$r(n,\mathrm{accuracy})=-0.700$ (95\% CI [-0.848, -0.355]).
Explanation prompting reduces but does not eliminate the trend. In this prompt
family, increasing $n$ both adds repeated participant lines and strengthens the
apparent consensus.

\paragraph{Explanation prompting reduces conformity.}
Adding an explanation requirement before the final answer improves robustness at
high social pressure. At $n=10$, explanation prompting reduces MMLU conformity
from 0.363 to 0.082 and increases accuracy from 0.637 to 0.867. On BBH, it
reduces conformity from 0.390 to 0.152 and increases accuracy from 0.610 to
0.808. The paired $n=10$ explanation effect is +0.230 on MMLU (95\% CI [0.136,
0.329], $p<0.001$) and +0.198 on BBH (95\% CI [0.090, 0.308],
$p<0.001$). The effect is smaller at low group sizes, where base conformity is
already low.

\paragraph{Question distillation is more consistent than devil's advocate
prompting.}
Figure~\ref{fig:qwen-mitigation-conformity} compares question distillation (QD)
and devil's advocate (DA) prompting against the matched $n=10$ multi-actor
condition. QD is strongest for MMLU base prompting, improving
accuracy by 0.276 (95\% CI [0.180, 0.375], $p<0.001$) and reducing conformity by
0.313 (95\% CI [-0.412, -0.221]). This MMLU QD condition has a lower parse
rate than the matched $n=10$ condition (0.810 versus 0.910), so its strict
accuracy gain is smaller. QD also reduces BBH base conformity by 0.200
(95\% CI [-0.300, -0.110]), although its accuracy gain is smaller and only
borderline under the paired permutation test (+0.110, 95\% CI [0.010, 0.210],
$p=0.063$). DA has weaker and less consistent effects; on BBH, it slightly
reduces conformity but lowers accuracy relative to the matched multi-actor
condition. QD preserves the fact of agreement while removing repeated answer
lines, so its improvement shows that repeated presentation contributes beyond
agreement alone.
