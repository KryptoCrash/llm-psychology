\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We evaluate Qwen on two controlled 100-item stimulus sets, one from MMLU and one
from BBH. Each item was selected by no-social-context screening, so the
experiment measures susceptibility to
incorrect peer answers rather than general benchmark accuracy. We report
accuracy as correct answers among parseable responses, and conformity as the
fraction of parseable responses that match the repeated incorrect peer answer.
Our multi-actor prompt format and the question distillation (QD) and devil's
advocate (DA) interventions follow \citet{zhu2025conformity}; the base multi-actor results
below are a replication of their central conformity finding on Qwen.

Unless otherwise noted, confidence intervals are 95\% item-bootstrap intervals
computed by resampling the 100 stimulus items with replacement. Paired contrasts
use the same resampled item indices in both conditions and paired permutation
tests over item-level correctness among examples parseable in both conditions.
These intervals quantify uncertainty over stimulus items conditional on the
observed generations and peer-answer assignments; they do not capture decoding-seed
variability.

\paragraph{Repeated incorrect answers induce conformity.}
Figures~\ref{fig:qwen-conformity-participants}
and~\ref{fig:qwen-accuracy-participants} summarize the standard multi-actor
condition. In base prompting, Qwen's accuracy falls sharply as incorrect peer
answers are repeated, while conformity rises. On MMLU, accuracy drops from 0.990
at baseline to 0.637 at $n=10$, with conformity rising to 0.363. On BBH,
accuracy drops from 0.970 to 0.610, with conformity rising to
0.390. The corresponding accuracy drops from baseline to $n=10$ are large under
base prompting: -0.353 on MMLU (95\% CI [-0.453, -0.256], $p<0.001$) and
-0.360 on BBH (95\% CI [-0.460, -0.260], $p<0.001$).
MMLU base prompting also becomes less parseable under repeated peer pressure:
the parse rate is 0.910 at $n=10$, so strict accuracy at this point is 0.580.
For BBH base prompting, the $n=10$ parse rate is 1.000.

\paragraph{Conformity scales with group size.}
Across the standard multi-actor conditions for $n=2,\ldots,10$, group size is strongly
positively correlated with conformity and negatively correlated with accuracy
(\ref{fig:qwen-conformity-participants}--\ref{fig:qwen-accuracy-participants}).
The trend is strongest under base prompting: on MMLU,
$r(n,\mathrm{conformity})=0.966$ (95\% CI [0.898, 0.985]) and
$r(n,\mathrm{accuracy})=-0.972$ (95\% CI [-0.984, -0.904]); on BBH,
$r(n,\mathrm{conformity})=0.846$ (95\% CI [0.700, 0.908]) and
$r(n,\mathrm{accuracy})=-0.700$ (95\% CI [-0.848, -0.355]).
Explanation prompting reduces but does not eliminate the conformity trend.

\paragraph{Explanation prompting reduces conformity.}
Adding an explanation requirement before the final answer improves robustness at
high social pressure. At $n=10$, explanation prompting reduces MMLU conformity
from 0.363 to 0.082 and increases accuracy from 0.637 to 0.867. On BBH, it
reduces conformity from 0.390 to 0.152 and increases accuracy from 0.610 to
0.808. The paired $n=10$ explanation effect is +0.230 on MMLU (95\% CI [0.136,
0.329], $p<0.001$) and +0.198 on BBH (95\% CI [0.090, 0.308],
$p<0.001$). The effect is smaller at low group sizes, where base
conformity is already low, but it becomes large once repeated peer pressure
accumulates.

\paragraph{Question distillation is more consistent than devil's advocate
prompting.}
Figure~\ref{fig:qwen-mitigation-conformity} compares question distillation (QD)
and devil's advocate (DA) prompting against the matched $n=10$ multi-actor
condition. QD is especially effective for MMLU base prompting, improving
accuracy by 0.276 (95\% CI [0.180, 0.375], $p<0.001$) and reducing conformity by
0.313 (95\% CI [-0.412, -0.221]). This MMLU QD condition has a lower parse
rate than the matched $n=10$ condition (0.810 versus 0.910), so the strict
accuracy gain is smaller than the parseable-conditioned gain. QD also reduces
BBH base conformity by 0.200
(95\% CI [-0.300, -0.110]), although its accuracy gain is smaller and only
borderline under the paired permutation test (+0.110, 95\% CI [0.010, 0.210],
$p=0.063$). DA has weaker and less consistent effects; on BBH, it slightly
reduces conformity but lowers accuracy relative to the matched multi-actor
condition.
