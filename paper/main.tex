\begin{abstract}
LLMs increasingly operate in settings where they observe answers from other
agents before responding. We study an Asch-style setting in which Qwen3-8B
answers MMLU and BBH items it solves without social context, then sees repeated
incorrect answers from prior ``participants.'' At ten participants, accuracy
falls from 0.990 to 0.637 on MMLU and from 0.970 to 0.610 on BBH, while
conformity to the repeated wrong answer rises to 0.363 and 0.390. Explanation
prompting and question distillation reduce conformity. Difference-of-means
directions comparing all-wrong peer contexts to random-peer-answer contexts are
identified by an activation oracle as incorrect-consensus pressure in a
late-middle layer band. At layer 23, MMLU projection scores increase with
participant count, and layer-wise cosine structure shows a coherent MMLU
contrast direction across layers 23--33. Activation steering with the layer-23
direction increases conformity on $n=3$ prompts and reduces it on $n=10$
prompts, giving causal evidence that this residual-stream direction contributes
to Qwen's conformity behavior.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Large language models are routinely placed in collaborative settings: they
summarize previous answers, review other agents' outputs, and participate in
multi-agent workflows. A model may know the answer in isolation, but abandon it
when an apparent group repeatedly states a different answer.

Prior work adapts classic social-psychology paradigms to language models
\citep{asch1951effects,zhu2025conformity} and finds conformity across knowledge
domains, with sensitivity to group size, unanimity, difficulty, source
characteristics, and uncertainty
\citep{bellina2026conformity,zhong2025disentangling}. These behavioral results
raise a mechanistic question: when a model conforms, can the social-pressure
manipulation be localized in internal representations?

We run a small but controlled replication and extension on Qwen3-8B. The
stimuli are 100 MMLU and 100 BBH items that Qwen answers correctly without
social context. This isolates cases where an incorrect social signal can pull
the model away from a reachable answer. We measure accuracy, parseability, and
conformity to the repeated wrong peer answer under group sizes
$n=1,\ldots,10$, with and without an explanation requirement, plus question
distillation and devil's-advocate mitigations from \citet{zhu2025conformity}.

We then examine Qwen's internal activations. Following work on contrastive
activation directions \citep{panickssery2024caa,haon2025vla}, we construct
difference-of-means directions between repeated-wrong and random-answer peer
contexts at the assistant-start token, then test these directions with
projection, layer-wise cosine analysis, and activation steering.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related-work}

\paragraph{Conformity and sycophancy.}
Asch's conformity experiments showed that a unanimous incorrect majority can
alter human judgments \citep{asch1951effects}. Recent work adapts this paradigm
to language models, finding that conformity varies with group size, unanimity,
difficulty, uncertainty, and source characteristics
\citep{zhu2025conformity,weng2025benchform,bellina2026conformity,zhong2025disentangling}.
Conformity is related to sycophancy, where models match a user's stated view
even when doing so conflicts with independent correctness
\citep{perez2023sycophancy,sharma2023sycophancy}.

\paragraph{Linear activation directions.}
Prior work has found behaviorally meaningful directions in language-model
activations for truth, honesty, refusal, and persona-like traits
\citep{burns2023discovering,marks2024geometry,arditi2024refusal,chen2025persona}.
Representation engineering and contrastive activation addition use
difference-of-means directions as population-level representations that can be
monitored or steered at inference time
\citep{zou2023representation,panickssery2024caa}. These methods motivate our
use of all-wrong-minus-random diffmeans as candidate directions for the social
pressure manipulation.

\paragraph{Activation explanation and causal tests.}
Linear directions require validation beyond diagnostic separability
\citep{alain2017understanding,belinkov2022probing}. We use activation oracles
for semantic localization \citep{karvonen2025activation} and activation
steering as a residual-stream intervention, following broader activation
patching and steering work
\citep{meng2022locating,wang2023interpretability,panickssery2024caa}.

\section{Discussion}

The behavioral results reproduce the central conformity pattern of
\citet{zhu2025conformity}: repeated incorrect peer answers can pull Qwen away
from answers it can otherwise produce. Explanation prompting sharply reduces
conformity at large group sizes, and question distillation helps most clearly
for MMLU base prompting.

The activation results give a more tentative mechanistic picture. The
all-wrong-minus-random contrast controls for much of the generic effect of
adding peer-answer text. The activation oracle marks residual-stream layers 10,
12, and 22--33 as pressure toward an incorrect consensus, while the layer-update
results point to layers 22--23 as contributing the conformity direction.
Projections onto the layer-23 direction increase with participant count within
MMLU, explanation prompting shifts condition means downward, and layers 23--33
share a high-cosine MMLU diffmean direction. Steering gives an intervention
check: adding the layer-23 direction increases conformity on $n=3$ prompts, and
subtracting it reduces conformity on $n=10$ prompts, with stronger suppression
on MMLU than BBH.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

The experiments use one open model, two selected 100-item stimulus sets, and one
stochastic generation per condition after retries. Confidence intervals quantify
item-level uncertainty only; they do not capture prompt paraphrase sensitivity,
seed-to-seed variability, model families, or arbitrary benchmark samples. The
selected-item design isolates social susceptibility but overstates baseline
accuracy relative to ordinary benchmark evaluation.

The activation oracle is itself a model component, and its labels are evidence
to inspect rather than ground truth. The projection and cosine analyses use
assistant-start diffmeans, so they can miss token-local mechanisms or more
distributed representations. Steering shows that the direction can influence
behavior under these interventions, but does not identify a complete mechanism.

\section{Reproducibility and ethics}
\label{sec:reproducibility}

The repository contains the generation scripts, analysis scripts, Qwen result
files, and generated CSV/JSON summaries used for the behavioral and activation
figures. Section~\ref{sec:causal-verification} reports the activation-steering
protocol and summary results. The experiments use public benchmarks and open
model weights; no human subjects or crowdsourced workers are involved.

The main ethical concern is deployment rather than data collection. The results
show that multi-agent or socially framed prompts can amplify a repeated wrong
answer, which is relevant to misinformation, biased consensus formation, and
automated decision pipelines that aggregate model outputs. The same mitigations
could be useful defensively, but exposing conformity measurements may also help
attackers design more persuasive prompts. We therefore frame the work as an
analysis of a failure mode and report limitations on generalization and control.
